History of Arithmetic

From Babylon to Baghdad

Arithmetic is humanity's oldest mathematical discipline. The Babylonians (c. 2000 BCE) developed a sophisticated base‑60 number system used to track grain stores, calculate land area, and predict celestial events. Clay tablets like the famous Plimpton 322 reveal they understood Pythagorean triples over 1,000 years before Pythagoras.

In the 9th century CE, the Persian polymath Muḥammad ibn Mūsā al-Khwārizmī wrote Kitāb al-mukhtasar fī ḥisāb al-jabr waʼl-muqābala, the treatise whose title gave us the word algebra. His earlier work On the Calculation with Hindu Numerals introduced the Hindu-Arabic numeral system (0–9) to the Islamic world and eventually Europe, replacing cumbersome Roman numerals. The very word algorithm is a Latinisation of al-Khwārizmī's name.

01

Integers

Integers are the complete set of whole numbers and their negatives: $\{\ldots,\,-3,\,-2,\,-1,\,0,\,1,\,2,\,3,\,\ldots\}$. They extend infinitely in both directions and contain no fractional part.

The absolute value $|x|$ measures distance from zero — it strips the sign:

Absolute Value
$$|x| = \begin{cases} x & \text{if } x \ge 0 \\ -x & \text{if } x < 0 \end{cases}$$

Signed arithmetic rules:

Adding a negative equals subtracting its positive: $\;5 + (-3) = 5 - 3 = 2$

Two negatives multiply to a positive: $\;(-4)\times(-3) = +12$

A positive times a negative gives a negative: $\;(-4)\times 3 = -12$

02

Fractions

A fraction $\dfrac{a}{b}$ represents the division of $a$ (the numerator) by $b$ (the denominator), where $b \ne 0$.

The Four Operations on Fractions
$$\frac{a}{b} + \frac{c}{d} = \frac{ad+bc}{bd} \qquad \frac{a}{b} - \frac{c}{d} = \frac{ad-bc}{bd}$$ $$\frac{a}{b} \times \frac{c}{d} = \frac{ac}{bd} \qquad \frac{a}{b} \div \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a}{b}\times\frac{d}{c} = \frac{ad}{bc}$$

When adding fractions, always find the lowest common denominator (LCD). For $\frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{6}$, the LCD is 12, giving $\frac{3}{12} + \frac{2}{12} = \frac{5}{12}$.

A fraction is in lowest terms when $\gcd(\text{numerator}, \text{denominator}) = 1$. For $\frac{6}{8}$: $\gcd(6,8)=2$, so $\frac{6}{8} = \frac{3}{4}$.

03

Decimals

Decimals express fractions using powers of 10. Each position to the right of the decimal point represents $10^{-1}$ (tenths), $10^{-2}$ (hundredths), $10^{-3}$ (thousandths), and so on.

Common Fraction–Decimal Conversions
$$\frac{3}{4} = 0.75 \qquad \frac{1}{3} = 0.\overline{3} \qquad \frac{7}{8} = 0.875 \qquad \frac{1}{6} = 0.1\overline{6}$$

A decimal terminates (e.g. $0.75$) when the denominator's only prime factors are 2 and 5. Otherwise it repeats (e.g. $0.\overline{3}$).

Converting a repeating decimal to a fraction: Let $x = 0.\overline{3}$. Then $10x = 3.\overline{3}$, so $9x = 3$ and $x = \frac{1}{3}$.

04

Order of Operations (PEMDAS)

When an expression contains multiple operations, a strict hierarchy determines which is evaluated first. Without it, $2 + 3 \times 4$ would be ambiguous.

PEMDAS Priority — highest to lowest
$$\underbrace{\mathbf{P}}_{\text{Parentheses}} \;\to\; \underbrace{\mathbf{E}}_{\text{Exponents}} \;\to\; \underbrace{\mathbf{MD}}_{\text{Mult / Div}\;(\text{left} \to \text{right})} \;\to\; \underbrace{\mathbf{AS}}_{\text{Add / Sub}\;(\text{left} \to \text{right})}$$

Worked trace: Evaluate $2 + 3 \times 4^2 \div (6-2)$

$\to$ Parentheses: $2 + 3 \times 4^2 \div 4$

$\to$ Exponents: $2 + 3 \times 16 \div 4$

$\to$ Mult/Div left to right: $2 + 48 \div 4 = 2 + 12$

$\to$ Addition: $= 14$

05

Percentages

A percentage expresses a number as a fraction of 100. The word derives from Latin per centum — "per hundred." The symbol % is shorthand for $\div 100$.

Core Percentage Formulas
$$\text{Percentage} = \frac{\text{Part}}{\text{Whole}} \times 100 \qquad p\%\ \text{of}\ x = \frac{p}{100} \times x$$ $$\text{Percentage change} = \frac{\text{New} - \text{Old}}{\lvert\text{Old}\rvert} \times 100$$

To find 35% of 80: $\;\frac{35}{100}\times 80 = 0.35 \times 80 = 28$.

Warning: A 20% increase followed by a 20% decrease does not return to the original: $1.2 \times 0.8 = 0.96$ — a net 4% loss. Order matters.

Real-Life Applications

💰
Personal Finance

Simple interest follows $I = P \cdot r \cdot t$, where $P$ is principal, $r$ is annual rate, and $t$ is years. Investing $\$1{,}000$ at 5% for 3 years earns $I = 1000 \times 0.05 \times 3 = \$150$.

Arithmetic underlies every financial decision: sales tax ($\text{price}\times 1.08$), discounts ($\text{price}\times(1-r)$), tipping, and comparing loan rates all rely on percentages and decimals.

🍳
Cooking & Scaling

Recipes are ratio problems in disguise. Scaling a recipe for 4 up to serve 10 requires multiplying each ingredient by $\frac{10}{4} = 2.5$. If the original calls for $\frac{2}{3}$ cup of sugar: $\;\frac{2}{3}\times 2.5 = \frac{5}{3} = 1\frac{2}{3}$ cups.

Unit conversions (oz → g, °F → °C) are linear arithmetic operations applied millions of times daily in kitchens and labs worldwide.

History of Algebra

From Rhetoric to Symbols

The word algebra derives from the Arabic al-jabr — "reunion of broken parts" — taken from the title of the 9th-century treatise by the Persian mathematician Muḥammad ibn Mūsā al-Khwārizmī. In that work he solved linear and quadratic equations entirely in prose, with no symbols at all.

Symbolic algebra emerged gradually. In the 16th century, the French mathematician François Viète introduced letters to represent known and unknown quantities. René Descartes later standardised the convention we still use today: letters near the end of the alphabet ($x, y, z$) for unknowns and letters near the beginning ($a, b, c$) for known constants. This seemingly small notational choice unlocked centuries of mathematical progress.

01

Variables and Expressions

A variable is a symbol (usually a letter) standing in for an unknown or changing quantity. An algebraic expression combines variables, constants, and operations — but contains no equals sign.

To evaluate an expression, substitute the given value for each variable and simplify following PEMDAS.

Evaluating an Expression
$$\text{If } f = 3x^2 - 2x + 1 \text{ and } x = 4{:}\quad f = 3(16) - 2(4) + 1 = 48 - 8 + 1 = 41$$

Like terms share the same variable(s) raised to the same power(s). Only like terms can be combined: $\;5x + 3x = 8x$, but $\;5x + 3x^2$ cannot be simplified further.

The distributive property lets you expand products over sums: $\;a(b + c) = ab + ac$.

02

Solving Linear Equations

A linear equation in one variable has the form $ax + b = c$ (where $a \ne 0$). Solving means isolating the variable using inverse operations — whatever is done to one side must be done to the other.

Properties of Equality
$$\text{If } a = b \text{, then:}\quad a + c = b + c \quad a - c = b - c \quad ac = bc \quad \frac{a}{c} = \frac{b}{c}\;(c \ne 0)$$

Strategy for multi-step equations:

1. Distribute and combine like terms on each side.

2. Move all variable terms to one side, constants to the other.

3. Divide by the coefficient of the variable.

Example: Solve $2(x + 3) - x = 11$: distribute → $2x + 6 - x = 11$ → combine → $x + 6 = 11$ → $x = 5$.

03

Systems of Linear Equations

A system is two or more equations that must be satisfied simultaneously. For two equations in two unknowns, there is generally exactly one solution — the point where their graphs intersect.

Standard Form of a 2×2 System
$$\begin{cases} a_1 x + b_1 y = c_1 \\ a_2 x + b_2 y = c_2 \end{cases}$$

Substitution method: Solve one equation for one variable, then substitute that expression into the other equation.

Elimination method: Multiply one or both equations by constants so that a variable's coefficients are opposites, then add the equations to eliminate that variable.

Example (elimination): $x + y = 7$ and $x - y = 3$. Add: $2x = 10$, so $x = 5$; substitute back: $y = 2$.

04

Inequalities

An inequality uses $<,\,>,\,\le,\,\ge$ instead of $=$. It describes a range of values rather than a single solution. Most rules for equations apply — with one critical exception.

The Flip Rule
$$\text{Multiplying or dividing both sides by a \textbf{negative} number \textbf{reverses} the inequality sign.}$$ $$-2x < 8 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; x > -4$$

Solutions are expressed as intervals: $x > 3$ on a number line has an open circle at 3 and an arrow pointing right. In interval notation: $(3,\,+\infty)$.

A compound inequality like $-1 \le 2x + 3 \le 9$ is solved by applying operations to all three parts simultaneously: subtract 3 → $-4 \le 2x \le 6$; divide by 2 → $-2 \le x \le 3$.

Real-Life Applications

⚙
Engineering & Science

Every physical law is an equation. Ohm's Law $V = IR$ lets an engineer solve for the unknown current when voltage and resistance are known. Newton's second law $F = ma$ is rearranged millions of times daily in structural and mechanical design to find the one unknown among three quantities.

Systems of equations model circuits with multiple loops (Kirchhoff's laws) or structural forces at a joint — the same algebraic techniques taught here scale directly to professional practice.

📈
Business & Economics

A company's break-even point is found by setting revenue equal to cost: $px = cx + F$, where $p$ is price per unit, $c$ is variable cost per unit, and $F$ is fixed costs. Solving: $x = \dfrac{F}{p - c}$ units must be sold to cover expenses.

Inequalities appear in budget constraints and linear programming — a business maximises profit subject to resource limits expressed as a system of inequalities.

Example 1
Solve: $3(x - 4) = 2x + 1$
Easy
1
Distribute the left side: $3x - 12 = 2x + 1$
2
Move variable terms left: subtract $2x$ from both sides: $x - 12 = 1$
3
Isolate $x$: add 12: $x = 13$
4
Verify: $3(13-4) = 3(9) = 27$ and $2(13)+1 = 27$ ✓
Answer: $x = 13$
Example 2
Solve the system: $2x + y = 7$ and $x - y = 2$
Medium
1
Elimination: add the two equations (the $y$ terms cancel):
$(2x + y) + (x - y) = 7 + 2 \;\Rightarrow\; 3x = 9$
2
Solve for $x$: $x = 3$
3
Back-substitute into $x - y = 2$: $3 - y = 2 \;\Rightarrow\; y = 1$
4
Verify in both equations: $2(3)+1=7$ ✓ and $3-1=2$ ✓

Example 3
Solve the compound inequality: $-4 \le 2x + 2 \le 10$
Hard
1
Subtract 2 from all three parts: $-4 - 2 \le 2x \le 10 - 2 \;\Rightarrow\; -6 \le 2x \le 8$
2
Divide all parts by 2 (positive, so no flip): $-3 \le x \le 4$
3
Interpret: any $x$ in the closed interval $[-3,\, 4]$ satisfies the inequality. Test $x = 0$: $-4 \le 2 \le 10$ ✓
Answer: $-3 \le x \le 4$, i.e.\ $x \in [-3,\, 4]$

History of Geometry

From the Nile to the Academy

The word geometry derives from the Greek geōmetria — "earth measurement." The ancient Egyptians used rope-stretchers (harpedonāptai) to re-survey farm boundaries after the annual Nile floods, a practical necessity that seeded one of mathematics' richest branches. The Rhind Mathematical Papyrus (c. 1550 BCE) contains area formulae for triangles and circles whose precision is remarkable for the era.

But it was the Greeks who transformed geometry from practical craft into rigorous science. Around 300 BCE, Euclid of Alexandria compiled Stoicheia (The Elements) — 13 books building all of plane and solid geometry from just 5 postulates and 23 definitions. So logically airtight was his deductive method that Elements served as a mathematical textbook for over 2,000 years. The 5th postulate — the parallel postulate — would remain controversial until the 19th century, when mathematicians finally built consistent non-Euclidean geometries by replacing it.

01

Points, Lines, and Angles

A point has no size — it marks a position. A line extends infinitely in both directions; a line segment connects two points; a ray starts at a point and extends in one direction.

An angle is formed by two rays sharing a common endpoint (the vertex). Angles are measured in degrees (°).

Angle Classification
$$\text{Acute}: 0° < \theta < 90° \quad \text{Right}: \theta = 90° \quad \text{Obtuse}: 90° < \theta < 180° \quad \text{Straight}: \theta = 180°$$

Complementary angles sum to 90°; supplementary angles sum to 180°. Vertical angles (formed by two intersecting lines) are always equal.

When a transversal crosses parallel lines, alternate interior angles are equal, and co-interior angles (same-side interior) are supplementary.

02

Triangles

Every triangle has three angles whose measures always sum to 180°. Triangles are classified by their sides (equilateral, isosceles, scalene) or their angles (acute, right, obtuse).

Key Triangle Formulas
$$\text{Angle sum: } \alpha + \beta + \gamma = 180°$$ $$\text{Area: } A = \tfrac{1}{2}\,b\,h$$ $$\text{Pythagorean theorem (right triangles only): } a^2 + b^2 = c^2$$

In a right triangle, the side opposite the right angle is the hypotenuse ($c$); the other two sides are the legs ($a$ and $b$). The Pythagorean theorem is the cornerstone of Euclidean geometry: if $a = 3$ and $b = 4$, then $c = \sqrt{9+16} = 5$ — the famous 3-4-5 right triangle.

The exterior angle theorem states that an exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles.

03

Quadrilaterals and Polygons

A polygon is a closed plane figure bounded by straight line segments. The interior angles of any $n$-sided polygon sum to $(n-2)\times180°$. A quadrilateral ($n=4$) therefore has angle sum $360°$.

Interior Angle Sum & Area Formulas
$$\text{Polygon angle sum: } S = (n-2)\times180°$$ $$\text{Rectangle: } A = lw \qquad \text{Parallelogram: } A = bh \qquad \text{Trapezoid: } A = \tfrac{1}{2}(a+b)h$$

Special quadrilaterals form a hierarchy: every square is a rectangle, every rectangle is a parallelogram, and every parallelogram is a trapezoid. A rhombus has four equal sides; a kite has two pairs of adjacent equal sides.

Example: A hexagon has $(6-2)\times180° = 720°$ total interior angle sum, so each interior angle of a regular hexagon is $720°\div6 = 120°$.

04

Circles

A circle is the set of all points in a plane equidistant from a fixed center. That distance is the radius $r$; the diameter is $d = 2r$. The ratio of circumference to diameter is always $\pi \approx 3.14159\ldots$

Circle Formulas
$$\text{Circumference: } C = 2\pi r = \pi d$$ $$\text{Area: } A = \pi r^2$$ $$\text{Arc length: } s = r\theta \quad (\theta \text{ in radians}) \qquad \text{Sector area: } A_s = \tfrac{1}{2}r^2\theta$$

A central angle has its vertex at the center; its measure equals the arc it intercepts. An inscribed angle has its vertex on the circle; its measure is half the intercepted arc.

A chord connects two points on the circle; the longest chord passes through the center and is the diameter. A tangent line touches the circle at exactly one point and is always perpendicular to the radius at that point.

05

Coordinate Geometry

The Cartesian plane — named after René Descartes — assigns each point a unique pair $(x, y)$. This bridge between algebra and geometry allows us to compute geometric properties using arithmetic.

Coordinate Geometry Formulas
$$\text{Distance: } d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$$ $$\text{Midpoint: } M = \left(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2},\; \frac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)$$ $$\text{Slope: } m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$

Two lines are parallel if they have equal slopes ($m_1 = m_2$) and perpendicular if their slopes are negative reciprocals ($m_1 \cdot m_2 = -1$).

The equation of a circle centered at $(h, k)$ with radius $r$ is: $(x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 = r^2$.

Real-Life Applications

🏛
Architecture & Engineering

Structural engineers use the Pythagorean theorem to verify right angles and calculate diagonal bracing. The stability of a triangular truss relies on the fact that a triangle is the only polygon whose shape is uniquely determined by its side lengths — it cannot be deformed without changing a side.

Architects use sector areas to design arched ceilings and domes; knowing $A_s = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta$ lets them calculate materials for curved surfaces with precision.

📍
Navigation & GPS

GPS satellites use the distance formula — generalised to 3D as $d = \sqrt{\Delta x^2 + \Delta y^2 + \Delta z^2}$ — to determine your location by trilateration from at least three satellites.

The great-circle distance (shortest path on a sphere) between two locations relies on spherical geometry, a natural extension of the coordinate geometry concepts introduced here. Every flight path optimisation depends on it.

Worked Examples

Example 1
A triangle has angles of 48° and 73°. Find the third angle.
Easy
1
Apply the angle sum theorem: the three angles of any triangle sum to 180°.
$48° + 73° + \theta = 180°$
2
Combine known angles: $48 + 73 = 121$
3
Solve for $\theta$: $\theta = 180° - 121° = 59°$
Answer: The third angle is $\mathbf{59°}$
Example 2
A ladder 13 m long leans against a wall. Its base is 5 m from the wall. How high up the wall does it reach?
Medium
1
Identify the right triangle: hypotenuse $c = 13$ m, one leg $a = 5$ m, unknown leg $b$ = height.
2
Apply Pythagorean theorem: $a^2 + b^2 = c^2 \;\Rightarrow\; 5^2 + b^2 = 13^2 \;\Rightarrow\; 25 + b^2 = 169$
3
Solve: $b^2 = 169 - 25 = 144 \;\Rightarrow\; b = \sqrt{144} = 12$
Answer: The ladder reaches $\mathbf{12}$ m up the wall. (Note: 5-12-13 is a Pythagorean triple.)
Example 3
Find the distance and midpoint between points $A(-3, 2)$ and $B(5, -4)$.
Hard
1
Distance formula: $d = \sqrt{(5-(-3))^2 + (-4-2)^2} = \sqrt{8^2 + (-6)^2} = \sqrt{64+36} = \sqrt{100} = 10$
2
Midpoint formula: $M = \left(\dfrac{-3+5}{2},\; \dfrac{2+(-4)}{2}\right) = \left(\dfrac{2}{2},\; \dfrac{-2}{2}\right) = (1,\; -1)$
Answer: Distance $= \mathbf{10}$ units; Midpoint $= \mathbf{(1,\,-1)}$
